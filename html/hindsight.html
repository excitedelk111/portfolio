Hindsight: Behavioral Analysis

March 2020: Brown announces that they will be closing for the rest of the semester because of the novel coronavirus. I am an international student, and all my American friends were heading back home, and I had to decide if I should fly back to India or continue to stay at Brown. I eventually decided to fly back, but that is not the decision I will be analyzing today. Instead, it’s the decision I had to take nearly a year later – whether or not I would come back to the US for the Spring semester.

December 2020: At this point, I had already spent nearly a year at home, and it’s not that I don’t love home or staying with family, but I missed my friends, many of whom I had grown extremely close to during my time at Brown. I also felt like I had already missed out on over ¼th of my college experience and did not want to miss out on more. Lastly, the near 11-hour time difference between India and the east coast meant staying up till 5 AM was now normal, but certainly not healthy.

On the other hand, coming back to the US would be expensive (flight tickets, health insurance, rent etc.), and travelling meant I could potentially get COVID. This would normally not be a problem since I am young and would just quarantine. However, I have asthma, and COVID being a respiratory infection, meant that it had a much higher chance of becoming more than just a simple fever and cough for me. I eventually decided to fly back to the US, and I am currently at an Airbnb in Columbus, Ohio having a great semester (all things considered) with three of my closest friends.

The first factor that made me consider not making this trip was that it would be significantly more expensive than if I just decided to do the semester remotely from home. These would be the approximate costs I would incur if I decided to come back: Flight Tickets ($2000), Health Insurance ($2500), Rent ($3000), Food ($2000), Miscellaneous ($1000). That’s nearly $10000 (give or take) – which is a lot of money. Since I decided to come back, I obviously convinced myself that this cost was worth it, and I think there are two psychological theories/hypotheses that explain how I justified this cost.

The first is a combination of anchoring and adjustment and the way I framed the situation for myself. Yes, $10000 is a lot of money when thought of as an absolute value independent of what it is being used for. However, if I valued my education at nearly $50000/year or $200000 in total, then $10000 is a seemingly marginal cost – just 5% of what I am totally spending on my education – when framed this way it doesn’t seem nearly as bad. I also think that $200000 acted as an anchor for what would be reasonable to spend on this “adventure” – it made the $10000 seem like a justifiable expense.

Second, I think that mental accounting played a significant part. I had spent nearly $60000 on my first year at Brown, while I had only spent $15000, so far, on my second year at Brown (since I had just paid the tuition fee for the fall semester). So, in my mind, it seemed reasonable to spend another $25000 on the spring semester ($15000 for tuition + $10000 for the rest) because it would bring the total to $40000 for the second year, which is $20000 less than what I spent the previous year.

In addition to which, I had secured an internship over the summer that was paying me almost $30000 and so I didn’t feel nearly as guilty about asking my parents to help me pay for this, since in my mental account, I used this $30000 to compensate for the second year’s costs bringing the effective cost for my second year down to $10000 compared to $60000 for my first year.

Coming to the second issue, which was in some ways, the more concerning one – my health. I knew that there was a good chance I could get COVID when travelling, and I think the best way to explain why this did not concern me as much as it should have, is the availability heuristic. I did not know anyone my age who had personally been infected I knew of one person with diabetes (one of the worst co-morbidities) who was infected and was totally okay. My asthma had never caused me any serious health concerns before this. I had friends who had flown back and forth to the US multiple times and not gotten infected.

Thus, from an availability perspective, it did not seem like I would ever get COVID (provided I took the necessary precautions), and even if I did, the consequences did not seem too bad, or at least they paled in comparison to what a seemingly normal semester would look like for me. Lastly, I know we tend to be risk-seeking in the domain of losses, and that clearly played a part here as well – I was willing to “take the gamble.”

To conclude, anchoring and adjustment, mental accounting, the availability heuristic and being risk-seeking in the domain of losses (the framing effect) certainly tricked me into thinking that the cons of this decision were not really “cons” - $10000 is a large amount of money (no matter what the circumstances are), and if I had gotten COVID, I could have permanently damaged my (already weakened) lungs. However, I think if I viewed this as a multi attribute decision and for example, used WADD, then I still would have made the same choice, because the memories I have made and will continue to make, and the friends and relationships I build now, will likely last me a lifetime – and if I realistically weighed the attributes involved in this decision, the weight of this single positive attribute would be sufficient to overcome the other negatively weighted attributes.
