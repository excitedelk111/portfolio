Short Essays On The Indus Valley Civilization

An Introduction

The Indus Valley Civilization lasted from about 3300 BCE to 1300 BCE and flourished between 2600 BCE and 1900 BCE. The region had five major urban centers or cities (> 80 Ha), all of which grew to considerable size and prominence over a relatively short period between 2600 BCE and 2500 BCE. I intend to primarily focus my investigations both on this period of development and subsequent years of flourishment. I will also focus on two of these five major cities, Mohenjo Daro and Harappa.

Before elaborating further on the specific reasons this region stood out to me, I must acknowledge that of equal importance, but not personal interest, is the rest of the region (and most of the population) that lived in smaller settlements (< 40 Ha) interspersed between the five major cities. This civilization was vast and spanned an area stretching from today’s northeastern Afghanistan, through much of Pakistan, and into western and northwestern India (Wright, 2009).

Naturally, the question arises, why would I have titled this page “Short Essays On The Indus Valley Civilization” when I just stated that I am only going to be focusing on Mohenjo Daro and Harappa, moreover, why would I choose to limit myself to just two cities given how large the civilization is. This is primarily because the rural context remains a poorly understood component of the civilization (Singh et al., 2009), both due to lack of remaining material culture and also due to the Indus Valley script not being decipherable.

Now that I have addressed perhaps the most obvious question, I will move on to the more interesting one. The first reason I wanted to focus on these cities was that they were noted for their particularly sophisticated urban planning, baked brick houses, elaborate plumbing and drainage systems, indoor toilets, water supply systems, and new techniques in handicraft and metallurgy (Wright, 2009). The civilization developed concurrently with ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia but was not just larger than the other two but also more advanced.

The second reason is the similarities in sophistication, city plan, construction materials (same type and shape of brick), and construction period between Mohenjo Daro and Harappa that seem to indicate that they had to be part of a unified government with extreme organization (Babul). However, at the same time, there is no overt evidence of prominent individuals from burials, sculptural traditions, or elite structures. This overlaps with unresolved questions about the nature of Indus elites, whether a ruling class dominated Indus cities, and whether there was significant monumental public architecture in the form of major religious buildings or palaces (Kenoyer, 2010). The lack of palatial or religious structures (Chakrabarti, 2008) and relatively uniform architecture (EORC) indicate that people were, for the most part, considered equals. This was especially intriguing to me because almost all ancient civilizations were the exact opposite. These facts also seemed to contradict each other as the cities were built quickly in a highly organized and advanced manner, yet all the people were equals and there was seemingly no clear power structure that could have organized this? It didn’t make sense.

The third and final reason was the lack of clear fortification and war preparedness. This furthers the argument that there were no clear power structures and that people lived as equals yet does nothing to explain the rapid construction of uniformly advanced cities spanning across a vast region. The cities did contain some minimal fortification; however, this was primarily near the “Citadel” where the Great Granary and Bath were found, not facilities of war (EORC). There is some evidence of knives, spears, and arrowheads, however, it is unclear if the weapons were used for protection against animals or for warfare (Babul). This seeming lack of war preparedness is also made further confusing by the fact that there is evidence of strong and consistent trade with Mesopotamia and the Persian Gulf area i.e., they were aware of potential “enemies” and conflicts, yet didn’t take any steps to protect themselves? Once again, it doesn’t make sense.

These facts are naturally intriguing, and our comprehension of these factors is irrevocably hampered by the fact that we are unable to decipher the script that might provide insight into the daily lives and practices of the people. However, I think it should be interesting for these very same reasons and I look forward to exploring these themes further, but I am also equally prepared to come to the conclusion that all we can do is speculate and that many of these interesting questions will remain unanswered.

The Foundations

With this essay I hope to explore what it meant for Mohenjo Daro to be considered one of the most advanced cities of its time with remarkably sophisticated civil engineering and urban planning (Wright, 2009). With that said, let’s jump right into it. The city could be split into two sections, a so-called upper acropolis or citadel and a lower city.

The citadel was built on the highest part of the city, likely to show the importance and status of buildings in that region. These buildings were largely for public use and were impressive from an architectural point of view. They were very well-planned with precise measurements and ratios. Some of the interesting ones are the so-called Granary and College. The Granary is impressive not because it is believed to have stored grain but because very few bronze-age cities in Eurasia had large storage structures like these; and the College because it is believed to be one of the earliest public educational institutions.

While the citadel was objectively impressive and probably meant to be a display of power and progress, the lower city primarily consisted of densely constructed rectilinear residential buildings on a grid plan (Pant and Fumo, 2005), with each block containing a network of houses and public wells. The lower city also featured urban elements such as roads that met at perfect right angles, hundreds of public wells, fire pits, and kilns, and were primarily functional in purpose rather than aesthetic (Iyengar, 2008). Evidence also shows that the Indus culture lacked magnificent buildings and on the contrary, most buildings were large-scale public buildings, commodious houses, or practical residences (Green, 2021).

As briefly mentioned previously, the citadel was built on the highest part of the city, this was not just for symbolic reasons but also to combat flooding from the Indus River. Walls were built around the entire city (with additional strength and thickness around the citadel), potentially to indicate combat strength and act as fortification but it was most likely to prevent flooding especially because there is evidence of tapering at the bottom of the walls to guide water away from the city.

The last notable feature of Harappan architecture, that I may explore in a subsequent essay in more detail, is that of a highly developed and sophisticated water and waste management system with hundreds of public wells extremely well integrated into the city. Experts acknowledge that even with today’s technology, this must have required advanced planning to implement, and it turns out that the city’s sewage, plumbing, and drainage systems were in fact distributed in the city grid plan by early hydro-engineers (Khan, 2020).

It clearly seems like Mohenjo Daro has the rightful title of one of the most advanced cities of its time, but this presses the question of how the civilization managed to achieve such sophistication without any clear hierarchical power structure and apparent equality of all its citizens. This question also intrigued me when I wrote the prior essay but only intrigues me more now.

For the sake of brevity, I won’t get into too much detail, but I think it will be interesting to consider the views of Massimo Vidale and Adam Green on this topic. What makes their arguments especially interesting is that they try to explain how the civilization managed to achieve such sophistication by exclusively investigating its architecture and planning i.e., conclusions are derived from what exists and not through speculation.

Vidale presents an argument for the presence of palace-like architecture supporting the argument that the Indus Civilization was headed by elites settled in individual, citadel-like walled enclosures competing for political supremacy (Vidale, 2010). This offers a simple and rational explanation to a natural question that is now no longer intriguing.

Green on the other hand, presents an argument for concepts of heterarchy, and collective action, offering potential explanations for how early state societies may have established high degrees of civic coordination in the absence of exclusionary political strategies or dominant centralized political hierarchies (Green, 2018). Since Green’s work was written in 2018, it is quite carefully constructed to make their point despite the evidence Vidale brings up. Green further backs up their argument with a more recent paper in 2021 where they argue that this was the first complex ancient society based on egalitarianism.

I may end up exploring these ideas in further detail in a future essay, and with that “cliffhanger,” I will conclude.

Water Management

This topic is particularly interesting because their water management system was more advanced than those of other civilizations prospering at the time and in many ways, resembles systems we have today. Ancient water management systems broadly seem to be influenced by three factors. The first is the region having too much water, which meant they had to protect themselves from it. The second is if it did not have much water at all, which meant they had to ensure they have enough to survive. The third is that of convenience and luxury, which influenced how the system was implemented. The Indus Valley’s system seems to be influenced by all three factors. The first two are naturally a result of the climate of the region, so we will start our discussion there.

There are two theories about climate in the region. According to the first theory, it used to be cold, wet, and rainy. In addition to which, there were constant floods during heavy rains and especially during snowmelt in the spring as the region is bounded by the Himalayas to the North. The livelihood of many people in the region depended on agriculture which in turn depended on these sources of water. The second theory says that the region experienced arid continental climate with hot long summers and short winters just like the climate of the region today (Hasan and Khan, 2010). This is supported by the fact that baked bricks were only used for construction on the riverbanks and not used in areas away from the river.

I personally think the two are not mutually exclusive. The Indus Valley Civilization was vast, and it makes perfect sense for cities like Mohenjo Daro close to the Indus River to have experienced conditions described by the first theory while cities like Dholavira that were much further inland, away from the river, and closer to the Thar desert, to have faced conditions described by the second theory.

Let’s first focus on Mohenjo Daro. As a result of its proximity to the Indus River, there was a constant risk of flooding; houses were built on plinths that rose above the street level with stairs recessed at the wall at the front door to avoid flooding (Khan, 2004). Most important buildings were situated on significantly higher ground in the Citadel (Nambiar, 2006) for the same reason. The city walls also show evidence of tapering at the bottom to guide water away from the city.

In addition to these protective measures, the town planners of Mohenjo Daro had also built the world’s first known drainage system. It was a central system that connected every household in the city and consisted of elaborate brick-lined channels for the removal of rainwater. Drains in the streets carried water from the city into the aforementioned covered channels that ran along the centers of the city's main streets into nearby agricultural fields, rivers, or streams. The entire system is of unparalleled engineering skill (Rothermund and Kulke, 1998). It’s important to note that this system also served as the sewage system and helped with waste management. I elaborate on this in my subsequent and final essay.

The last point I will touch on is the influence of convenience and luxury. They were known for their obsession with water (Jansen, 1989), and were masters at constructing wells. It is estimated that about 700 wells were built within the city, an average of one well for every third house, and the bigger houses had their own wells (Jones, 1967). These wells were constructed with tapering bricks that were strong enough to last for centuries (Ann, 2009).

Before moving onto Dholavira, let’s briefly explore the region’s irrigation systems. These systems had to be robust because, as previously mentioned, large scale agriculture was practiced as livelihood by many in the region; and they had developed an extensive network of canals for their irrigation needs. It was reasonably sophisticated and in practice was very similar to the canal systems developed in southern Mesopotamia. The Indus Valley canals were also supported by large reservoirs, such as the ones built at Girnar (Shirsath, 2009).

While there were strict public maintenance rules for canals in Mesopotamia and less strict but still community-based maintenance systems in later civilizations like Rome, it’s not clear how any of the water management systems (including the irrigation system) in the Indus Valley region, were constructed or maintained. The standardized nature of the systems as well as provisions for regular inspection of drains seem to indicate that it was maintained by authorities, but as I’ve previously mentioned, authority and governance structures are a major point of contention in this region. Given how important water is to the functioning of any society (including our own today), it once again begs the question of whether such organized use of irrigation and water management systems allowed centralized authority to rise or if centralized authority was necessary to organize such systems in the first place – certainly interesting to think about, but somewhat vain to discuss due to the lack of evidence to support either argument.

Getting back to our primary discussion, now moving onto Dholavira. Given its location in present day Rann of Kutch, a salt marsh in the Thar desert, and the ensuing climate of the region, it was necessary to collect and store water for various purposes. The Archaeological Survey of India has revealed that one third of the area of the city was devoted to collection and distribution of fresh water. So, it’s clear that this was very important to them, but how exactly did they use all that space?

They had a sophisticated water conservation system of channels and reservoirs, the earliest found anywhere in the world. Massive reservoirs situated around the metropolis were fortified with stonewalls and were used for storing freshwater brought by rains and water diverted from two nearby rivulets. There were sixteen or more such reservoirs of varying size. Like the Nabateans in Petra that we discussed in class, a lot of these channels and reservoirs took advantage of the natural environment, specifically the slope of the ground within the large settlement, a drop of 13m from northeast to northwest.

In addition to these massive reservoirs, there were several wells and tanks. Many tanks were cut directly into rock to provide drinking water to tradesmen who used to travel along the trade route. Each local settlement in the area had its own water harvesting and storage system in the form of rock-cut cisterns, ponds, tanks and wells, some of which are still in use today. This is once again a reflection of the priority placed on water by the civilization, the so-called “convenience and luxury” factor I previously mentioned.

I think this quote summarizes the thoughts of most scholars of the region, reflects why the Harrapans’ water management systems were worth discussing, and is a perfect way to conclude, "The kind of efficient system the Harappans of Dholavira developed for conservation, harvesting and storage of water speaks eloquently about their advanced hydraulic engineering, given the state of technology at the time" (Subramanian, 2010).

Waste and Sanitation

The way a civilization treats waste and sanitation is a direct reflection of how civilized they are. This is a strong claim and should intuitively make sense but let me elaborate. The quality of systems to deal with waste and sanitation are directly proportional to public health outcomes, and the value a society places on human life is directly proportional to how civilized they are. For those still skeptical, simply consider exactly why we strive to advance the field of medicine, why life expectancy is an indicator of developed countries, and most recently, why we “stopped” the world and decided to wear masks, but didn’t in 1918, at least not to the extent we did in 2020.

Further, more advanced waste management and sanitation systems allow a society to support and sustain larger populations, as was the case with the Indus Valley Civilization. More recent and concrete examples of why this is important are illustrated by the challenges that London and New York faced with rising populations, just at a much larger scale than the Indus Valley Civilization. In London, with increasing urbanization of the population in the nineteenth century, filthy environmental conditions became common in working class areas, and the spread of disease became rampant. Smallpox, cholera, typhoid, and tuberculosis reached unprecedented levels. It was estimated that nearly 10% of the population died of smallpox, and more than half the working class died before their fifth birthday. In New York, as late as 1865, filth and garbage accumulated in the streets to a depth of two or three feet; as a result of which, spread of diseases was rampant and child mortality rates were at all-time highs.

This is all relevant to highlight the importance of waste management and sanitation, as well as why Mohenjo Daro’s system was especially impressive and a reflection of the advancement of their society. Mohenjo Daro presents a picture of a community in which both personal and community cleanliness was effectively practiced, and the water supply reasonably safeguarded from contamination. This was possible because early engineers created sophisticated plumbing and sewage systems that were well integrated into city plans. These systems could rival any urban drainage systems built before the 19th century (Khan et al., 2020), and meant that most people lived a luxurious life with proper sanitation (Saleem, 2012).

Practically every house in Mohenjo Daro had its own bathroom (Mulchandani and Shukla, 2010), always placed on the street side of the building for convenient disposal of wastewater into street drains; these drains are the same ones that feed into the centralized drainage system briefly discussed in the previous essay. The drains were usually 46 to 61 cm below street level and varied in depth and width from 23cm to 30cm (Gray, 1940). When the drain could not be covered by flat bricks, or stone slabs, the roof of the drain was corbelled.

In the bathroom, people stood on a brick “shower tray” with raised edges and tipped water over themselves from a jar. The clean water came from a well as nearly all houses had excellent plumbing facilities for provision of water (Rothermund and Kulke, 1998); and as previously mentioned, the wastewater drained through a terracotta pipe out through the wall into the drain on the street (Lofrano and Brown, 2010).

Latrines were also commonly found inside houses and were once again, placed on the street wall for the same reason as the bathrooms; what’s also interesting is that latrines were placed immediately adjacent to the bathrooms, exactly like we do today in the modern world, and for the same reason – ease of plumbing. The latrines were designed exactly like modern day western-style toilets, made from bricks with wooden seats on top and were flushed with water from jars (Hooper, 2011). There were also instances of bathrooms and latrines located on the upper floors of larger buildings, and they were drained by vertical terracotta pipes with closely fitting spigot joints, set in the building wall.

The centralized drainage system briefly discussed in the prior essay, and something I promised to elaborate on, is an engineering marvel in and of itself. It connected every household in the city and consisted of waterproof brick-lined channels that ran along the centers of the city's main streets carrying water and waste from the city into closed seeps or directly out of the city (Jansen, 1985). In order to prevent the drains from clogging up, a wooden screen at the end of the drains held back solid waste. The drains had inspection holes to regularly check on the status of the drains and had movable stone slabs for maintenance. The liquid wastes either went straight out of the city or entered a cesspool made of radial bricks. These cesspools were regularly cleaned out.

In addition to the impressive sewage system, it appears that houses also had trash chutes built into the walls, sometimes even descending from upper floors, at the foot of which there were bins at the street level. It’s speculated that those who cleaned the drains and cesspools were also responsible for clearing trash from the bins on the sides of houses (Khan, 2004). Public trash bins were also provided at convenient places (Gray, 1940). This sort of system is very familiar to us today in the modern world.

Speaking of public trash bins, public toilets were also present despite the extensive private toilet system. These were primarily found in government buildings and multiple dwelling houses which ranged from single to several stories with many rooms, public baths, common wells, and sanitation facilities (Kuthiala, 2009). These facilities were maintained extremely well; this is not much of a surprise given their society’s emphasis on sanitation and hygiene, but this is unlike most public toilets, be it Vespasianae from the past or even today, where there is a sense of disgust with the poor maintenance, vandalism and lack of basic facilities (Pathak, 1995).

Concluding with what I started off with, I think there is something to be said for the fact that in other contemporaneous civilizations, skills of the artist and builder were lavished upon temples, palaces and homes of the elite while the homes of the common man were inadequate and could be considered ephemeral hovels. It’s clear that there was something different about the priorities of the people of the Indus Valley and I think it is extremely interesting to see people today advocating for that same equity, just differently, with things like universal basic income, and equitable housing and healthcare. Maybe history does repeat itself.
