Searching for Extraterrestrial Intelligence

Introduction

This essay will provide a brief analysis of what the Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence (SETI) means, what it is motivated by, what it entails, and what its chances of success are. It starts off by delving into a discussion about the first question by providing summaries of prominent views on the definitions of life and intelligence while simultaneously justifying why the search for intelligence is more appealing than that for life. After arriving at a reasonable consensus, our motivations for exploring the universe and searching for extraterrestrial intelligence are discussed – both philosophical and logical arguments are presented alongside rationale for why this search isn’t a wild goose chase. Lastly, the paper concludes with a presentation of 3 of the primary approaches used to search for extraterrestrial intelligence, appropriately balancing justifications for such approaches and details on the techniques themselves. A consistent theme of the essay will be to justify the rationality of this endeavor thereby providing sufficient intuition for the readers themselves to think about the last explicitly unanswered question – what are our chances of success with this search?

Some Basic Definitions

Defining Life

As we start exploring what the search for extraterrestrial intelligence means, naturally, a few fundamental questions arise. How do we define extraterrestrial intelligence? Does intelligence necessarily require life? If so, how do we define life? Let’s start by exploring our definition of life. While this may seem like a philosophical question, it is one that has more recently been explored in excessive detail by astrobiologists as the need to provide a clear definition for life has taken on increasing significance in recent years. Relevant to our context is the fact that as we explore more of our solar system and the galaxy, we will encounter extraterrestrial systems that may resemble life and we will need to determine if the system satisfies a set of properties that fall within an accepted definition of life. As may be apparent, this is a hard question to answer, so I will simply provide an overview of some of the most prominent views on this topic.

Deamer5 suggests stating a minimal set of properties associated with the living state, that, taken together, exclude anything that is not alive. With Deamer’s5 list of properties we run into a classical issue with a strict definition of life which is that there are often robust counterexamples and when properties are excluded one at a time, the definition becomes exponentially weaker. The following simple example illustrates this. Suppose a system reproduced perfectly so that evolution could not occur, let’s assume this meets our definition of life. Consider another system in which all the nutrients required for growth were present in the medium so that no metabolism was required, let’s assume this does not meet our definition of life. Viruses, have no metabolism – they require the cytoplasm of cells to survive – however, they can also evolve, so are they life or not? 

On the other hand, Tirard16, Morange16 and Lazcano16 suggest that while phenomenological characterizations of life are feasible, a precise definition of life remains an elusive intellectual endeavor. They claim that instead of viewing life vs non-life as a discrete distinction, the emergence of life be seen as the stepwise (but not necessarily slow) evolutionary transition between the non-living and the living – so, it would be meaningless to draw a strict line between them, especially without any context of the environment in which they evolve.

Bedau2 bridges the gap between these views stating that we can attribute a significant part of the controversy over the definition of life to the use of a Cartesian approach to explaining life, which seeks necessary and sufficient conditions for being an individual living organism, out of the context of other organisms and the abiotic environment. He takes on an Aristotelian approach to defining life, not philosophically, but through the Program-Metabolism-Container (PMC) which provides a definition for minimal chemical life – explaining eight of life's hallmarks, one of life's borderline cases (the virus), and two of life's puzzles. These papers were written in 2010 and were the primary ones analyzed as they were present in NASA’s “Defining Life: A Collection of Essays”.

A far more recent paper from Bartlett1 and Wong1 proposes an interesting term “lyfe” with four classifying standards (dissipation, autocatalysis, homeostasis, and learning) instead of the traditional eight (homeostasis, organization, metabolism, growth, adaption, response to stimuli, and reproduction). Their goal was to solve current controversies on the definition of life and broaden our criteria for life-search on other planets. Peer reviews seem to suggest that while their analysis is far-sighted and insightful in some ways, it is seemingly just a summary of the classical definition of life; and the authors agree, stating that their intent was to come up with a generalized framework that would not fall prey to the often-robust counterexamples that break all prior definitions of life. This paper, however, is very recent and requires deeper review. 

I will stop here since this is not the primary focus of this essay but simply serves to illustrate the complexity of defining life, and thus, the search for it. It is also meant to serve as a precursor to why attempting to define intelligence and searching for intelligence instead of life may be a more appealing endeavor.

Defining Intelligence

We can now explore the more relevant and interesting questions posed at the start of this section. How do we define extraterrestrial intelligence? Does intelligence necessarily require life? Let’s start by exploring our definition of intelligence, and for the moment, just assume that intelligence does not necessarily require life – exactly why will become clearer as we explore our definition of intelligence.  Conventionally, extraterrestrial intelligence is often conflated with the concept of sapience i.e., extraterrestrial life with human-like abilities. An alternative and also often cited definition is that of sentience, which requires intelligence but not life, yet in my opinion, this is still a far too complex definition of intelligence for our purposes; or more accurately, we are not really searching for intelligence itself but rather for signs of intelligence.

Any observation in the universe that appears unnatural may be considered a sign of preliminary intelligence – I say preliminary because it may appear unnatural to us but could end up being a natural occurrence – just one we have not previously encountered (as we realized was the case with pulsars). It is important to note that we are only assuming that searching for any unnatural occurrences in the universe and using those as signs of intelligence is a reasonable way to search for extraterrestrial intelligence; and the reason we can make this assumption is because we can make fairly accurate distinctions between the naturally occurring and not. I say this because in as much of our galaxy and the universe that we have explored we have not found a single chemical element we have not previously identified, including in galaxies that are not our own. 

To provide a more concrete example to drive my point home, say we identify a metal object with a very specific geometric pattern (a hypothetical intergalactic communication beacon) that is the sole unnatural object on an otherwise uninhabitable planet – we have most certainly detected the presence of an extraterrestrial intelligence – but we have done so not through detecting life or life with intelligence but rather through a sign of intelligence. While I couldn’t find a paper that explicitly defined extraterrestrial intelligence or the search for it in the way I summarized above, most prominent papers, including Sagan14 and Drake’s14 famous The Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence and Cocconi21 and Morrison’s21 Searching for Interstellar Communications allude to the fact that they define extraterrestrial intelligence this way. 

Since this is not a comprehensive analysis on the definition of extraterrestrial intelligence, let’s conclude this discussion by assuming we have a reliable definition of extraterrestrial intelligence (or at least whatever it is that we are looking for) i.e., we will know when we have found “it” and we will “all” agree on it. Like I alluded to previously, defining intelligence (or signs of intelligence) was a far easier task than doing so for life, and assuming that intelligence does not require life does not invalidate our search for intelligence, it only makes it easier.

Our Motivation

Now we can move onto the second of four primary questions I wanted to explore. What motivates our search for extraterrestrial intelligence? There is a philosophical motivation which is that we seem to care enough about our place in the hierarchy of all intelligent beings to share knowledge about ourselves with others and to hope that others (if they exist) would want to do the same. I made a subtle assumption when I said “all intelligent beings” – I assumed that there are others out there – to skeptics, and quite frankly, most people, this may seem like an outlandish assumption. This is almost certainly because they don’t truly understand the scale involved when talking about things in the universe – both in terms of space and time. So, I will now provide some empirical evidence to both provide context for those who don’t have any and to justify why this assumption, is in fact, rational. 

The sun formed 10 billion years ago, life on Earth started 3.5 billion years ago, homo sapiens arrived 300,000 years ago, humans left the first recorded signs of abstract and symbolic thought 40,000 years ago and signs of intelligence, at the very earliest, have been detectable (from space) for the last century or so. It is clear that despite the apparent 3.5 billion years it took for intelligence (and technology) to arrive on Earth (after life first emerged) there are obvious selective advantages to it, at least up to the present evolutionary stage when technology also brings the threats of ecological catastrophes, the exhaustion of natural resources and nuclear war. Barring such disasters, we can reasonably expect that humans will remain on the earth for at least millions, if not billions of years and considering the exponential technological progress in the last century, it would no longer seem illogical to consider technological advancements capable of allowing interstellar communication and/or travel in the near future (a few thousand years, maybe less). In addition to which, we know that there are 100 billion stars in our galaxy alone (and 200 billion galaxies in the universe), and at least one planet for every star i.e., there are at least 100 billion planets in our galaxy. These facts alone should make our search for intelligence seem rational. 

However, we can take this a step further, let’s assume the probability of life is very low and that the number of individual steps required for the evolution of intelligence and technology so large and improbable that not all inhabited planets evolve technical civilizations, and even if it were highly likely that civilizations tended to destroy themselves at about our level of technological development, the number of technological civilizations in the galaxy today would still be very large. Sagan14 and Drake14 estimate there being around a million civilizations (0.001% of all planets) in our galaxy at or beyond the earth's present level of technological development. Even a skeptic would agree that there is a possibility that 0.01% of Sagan14 and Drake’s14 estimation i.e., 100 civilizations (0.00001% of all planets), may be a rational estimate, and (at least) I believe that if there are 100 intelligent civilizations out there (in our galaxy alone), then it is certainly worth looking for them. I hope I have managed to convince you, with this brief argument, that this is not a wild goose chase and is in fact a rational effort. 

To get back to our motivation, my initial philosophical argument stands, but if one wants to take a more concrete stance, consider that of Jill Tarter who says that at least part of our motivation should be that it is almost certain that any intelligence we come into contact with will be far more advanced than us and if we can convince them to travel to us, we should do so, and if they are able to, then they probably have everything they need in their own galaxy so they would only come to help us, and we need the help.

Before I conclude this discussion, as an aside, I must acknowledge Fermi’s Paradox – the apparent contradiction between high estimates of the probability of the existence of extraterrestrial civilizations and humanity’s lack of contact with, or evidence for, such civilizations. I will point out that considering the size of the universe, 100 (civilizations) isn’t a high number so we shouldn’t expect to have found even 1 in such a short period of time. In addition, the great filter hypothesis18, the rare earth hypothesis19, and the zoo hypothesis20 are just few of many possible explanations for the lack of evidence (we have been able to gather) for extraterrestrial life/intelligence. 

The Search

Now that we have established exactly what we are searching for and why we would be searching for it, we get to the questions of how we are conducting this search and what our chances of success could be. I will briefly touch on 3 of the primary methods we use to search for extraterrestrial intelligence today. The first is detection of radio signatures, the second is detection of techno signatures and lastly, I will briefly touch on search techniques based on some purely hypothetical theories (Kardashev scale, Dyson spheres and Shkadov thrusters).

Radio Signatures

The detection of radio signatures has been, by far, the longest continuous effort and the one we have invested the most resources into. Cocconi21 and Morrison’s21 classic Searching for Interstellar Communications written in 1959 makes a strong case for the detection of radio signatures and Sagan14 and Drake’s14 equally famous The Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence nearly 40 years later in 1997, does the same again. So, why do we expect there to be interstellar radio signatures? Given the vast distances between planets in the universe, any communication would happen only between very long-lived and patient civilizations. For some context, if we make Sagan14 and Drake’s14 assumption of there being a million civilizations in our galaxy at or beyond the earth's present level of technological development and if we assume these civilizations are distributed randomly throughout space then the distance between us and the nearest civilization should be about 300 light-years. So, any information conveyed between the nearest civilization and our own will take a minimum of 300 years for a one-way trip and 600 years for a question and a response (another argument against Fermi’s Paradox since we’ve only had radio technology for about half a century). In view of these facts, which should be common to and deducible by all intelligent civilizations in our galaxy, it seems quite likely that one-way radio messages are being beamed at the earth at this moment. 

The prior argument assumes that most other civilizations are at around the same level of technological development as we are. However, this is almost certainly not the case since we have only achieved the capability for interstellar radio communication in the past few decades, it is almost certain that any civilization we encounter will be far more advanced than us. If this is the case, then it may be reasonable to expect that to an advanced extraterrestrial civilization that is surveying the galaxy for signs of life, at some point in time, the Sun, and its planets (or at least two of them – Earth and Mars), would have met the criterion for harboring life. It is reasonable to make this assumption because even our “primitive” technology would be capable of telling us this if we were in another star system and were looking at the solar system. So, a more advanced civilization can be expected to know at least this much if not much more. In expectation of the development of life and intelligence on one of these planets, it is highly likely that any extraterrestrial civilization would set up a channel of communication that we would one day discover. Ordinarily, what I just said would seem like science-fiction, yet I hope by this point, it does not, but rather seems perfectly rational.

I’ve tried my best to make a convincing argument for why we should be searching for interstellar radio signatures, but really, I’ve just been making an argument for why we should be searching for interstellar communications. Why radio signatures? Why not some other means of communication? The answer is actually quite simple, interstellar communication across the galactic plasma without dispersion in direction and flight-time is practical and financially feasible only with electromagnetic radiation. Interstellar space vehicles cannot be excluded a priori, but in nearly all cases (no matter the extent of technological advancement) they would be a slower, more expensive, and more difficult means of communication. This argument is further bolstered by the fact that any civilization, no matter where it exists, must contend with the same laws of physics, astronomy, and radio technology that we do – so they too must come to the same conclusions as us. 

Now that we have convinced ourselves that searching for radio signatures is a rational and promising means of detecting extraterrestrial intelligence we must still guess or deduce the frequency at which the signal is being sent, the width of the frequency band, the type of modulation and the star transmitting the message. Cocconi21 and Morrison21 make an argument for searching at the frequency of the spin-flip transition of hydrogen at 1,420 megahertz, while Sagan14 and Drake14 say that such a channel may be too noisy for communication precisely because hydrogen, the most abundant interstellar gas, absorbs and emits radiation at that frequency. The truth is that the number of other plausible and available communication channels is not large since most of the astronomical radio spectrum is quite noisy, so deducing the right one should not be too difficult, and we’ve probably got hundreds, if not thousands of scientists and engineers working on this every time we launch multi-billion dollar telescopes. 

This is only a brief overview of radio signature detection and instead of getting into the technical details of exactly how we do this, I felt it was more important to explain why doing so made sense in the first place. For the sake of brevity, I will conclude here, but as with most things in this essay, this topic alone could be explored as a separate book.

Techno Signatures

The search for techno signatures is a far more recent endeavor – one that we have not invested too many resources into and also one that is a little more nebulously defined than the search for radio signatures. First, let’s define a techno signature. In essence, it is any measurable property or effect that provides scientific evidence of past or present technology. This is very closely related to my argument for how we should be defining extraterrestrial intelligence in the first place. Some examples of techno signatures include radiation leakage from mega scale astroengineering structures such as Dyson spheres, artificial light pollution from an extraterrestrial ecumenopolis (an entire planet that is a single city), or Shkadov thrusters (stellar engines that harness a star’s energy for interstellar travel). I will first focus specifically on the detection of atmospheric pollution and artificial heat/light pollution as potential techno signatures. As I stated earlier, I will touch on Dyson spheres and Shkadov thrusters in the following section on search techniques based on some purely hypothetical theories. 

Lin22, Abad22, and Loeb22 make a case for why the detection of industrial pollution serves as a chemical signature of intelligent life. Their paper focuses on harnessing the abilities of the soon-to-be launched James Webb Space Telescope which will give us unprecedented insights into the atmospheres of exoplanets similar (in size and proximity to the host star) to Earth by allowing us to compare a star’s spectrum as-is to when an exoplanet transits in front of the star allowing us to see absorption lines corresponding to the planet’s atmosphere. They decide to specifically focus on 2 CFCs – CCl3F (trichlorofluoromethane) and CF4 (tetrafluoromethane) despite methane and nitrous oxide, which are also byproducts of industrialization, being easier to detect. This is primarily because methane and nitrous oxide are not as exclusively anthropogenic as CFCs. Since CFCs are harder to detect, we can only detect very high levels (much higher than the levels of such pollutants on Earth). The authors found that ~1.2 days of total integration time will be needed to detect ~10 times the amount of CCl3F and ~1.7 days to detect ~10 times the amount of CF4 as there is on Earth. 

There are 2 caveats to using this method of detection. The first is that since these pollutants have very long lifespans, CF4, for example, has a half-life of around 50,000 years, if an exoplanet’s atmosphere showed high levels of these pollutants without signs of shorter-lived biomarkers, we might have evidence that an intelligent civilization once resided on that planet but does not anymore. It is quite ironic that one of the worst-case scenarios for pollution in Earth’s atmosphere makes for the best-case scenario for atmospheric detection of an alien civilization. The second is that the telescope exposure times needed to detect these industrial pollutants are much greater than what it will take to find water vapor, molecular oxygen, carbon dioxide, and other basic indicators of unintelligent life. However, if we first detect water vapor, molecular oxygen, carbon dioxide, and other basic biomarkers including methane and nitrous oxide, they can give us a hint as to whether industrial pollution may be present. If these biomarkers and pollution indicators are found, it may be worth the extra time to determine levels of CFCs. This longer timeframe needed to detect CFCs also allows for detecting additional biomarkers like the chlorophyll “red edge”. This paper, of course, is offering a very specific approach to detecting intelligent life via industrial pollution, but it is the only one grounded in facts and with a concrete plan as to how it can be performed. 

There are, however, 2 drawbacks to this approach. The first being that we are now looking for intelligent life and not just signs of intelligence (which certainly narrows our chances of positive detection) and the second that there is always a possibility of misdetection; for example, the atmosphere of Titan (Saturn’s largest satellite) has detectable signatures of complex chemicals that are similar to industrial pollutants, though certainly not the byproduct of civilization. It may seem like the argument for detecting industrial pollution as a techno signature is quite weak, but it turns out that it is even weaker for artificial heat/light pollution.

When it comes to the detection of artificial heat/light pollution, Avi Loeb and Edwin Turner have proposed that artificial light from extraterrestrial planets, such as that originating from cities, industries, and transport networks, could be detected and signal the presence of an advanced civilization. This assumes that the radiant energy generated by a civilization would be relatively clustered and can therefore be detected easily. 

However, this is not necessarily the case. We need a way of showing that light and heat detected from planets can be distinguished from natural sources to conclusively prove the existence of intelligent life. This may turn out to be harder than expected as NASA's 2012 Black Marble experiment showed that significant stable light and heat sources on Earth turn out to be naturally occurring and originating from uninhabited areas – chronic wildfires in arid Western Australia was one such spot identified by this mission. Clearly, the detection of artificial heat/light pollution is a far less conclusive means of detecting intelligence than industrial pollution and also has the same drawback which is that we are now looking for intelligent life and not just signs of intelligence.

Searching for techno signatures is certainly interesting and is a step in the right direction but it should be clear that our approach needs to be refined to search for signs of intelligence (and not intelligent life) and we need to come up with more concrete ways of doing so – right now, it is far too nebulous and loosely defined to justify investing significant resources into it. Once again, this is a new and burgeoning means to detecting intelligence and I can’t even get close to doing it justice with what I have said here but it should serve to at least provide a brief introduction to the space and some justification as to why radio signatures are still our best bet.

Kardashev Scale, Dyson Spheres and Shkadov Thrusters

To conclude this discussion, let’s briefly touch on some search techniques based on purely hypothetical theories – simply because they are interesting. The Kardashev23 scale was first put forth in 1964 and classified all civilizations as Type I, II or III (it has since been expanded to include types IV and V). Type I civilizations can harness all the resources of their home planet (1016 – 1017 W), Type II civilizations can harness all the energy from their star (4 x 10­26 W) and Type III civilizations can harness all the resources of their galaxy (4 x 1037 W). At its simplest level this is what the classification means but it is also meant to serve as a measure of intelligence as measured by transmission of information. For some context as to where we stand, Sagan introduced a continuous Kardashev scale in 1973 where a value was assigned to a civilization based on the amount of energy they consumed and Earth’s energy consumption in 2008 gave us a score of 0.7 – so we’ve got some progress to make before we even get classified as Type I. The Kardashev scale actually gives us context when discussing Freeman Dyson’s24 ideas in his famous paper Search for Artificial Stellar Sources of Infrared Radiation which was interestingly, independent of Kardashev’s ideas, published earlier, in 1960.

Dyson believed that if extraterrestrial intelligence exists and has reached a high level of technical development, at a certain point (as they transition from Type I to II), they will construct a hypothetical megastructure or swarm of satellites that completely encompasses a star and manages to capture most of its energy. This structure is called a Dyson sphere/swarm. If such structures existed, they could affect the characteristics of their host stars/galaxies. Some of the possible techno signatures of such astroengineering projects include reduced optical luminosity, boosted infrared luminosity and morphological anomalies. Dyson specifically focused on the infrared radiation, but later reserach25 explores some of the other effects by investigating predicted offsets of such galaxies/stars from the optical Tully-Fisher relation. 

To some, this may seem completely like science fiction, maybe even so far as to feel insulted that it was mentioned in this essay, and such a sentiment may be valid for Shkadov thrusters but they are analyzed regardless, simply because, at least I, think they’re quite cool; but getting back to why Dyson spheres made it in here, it’s primarily because we have a scientifically sound way of detecting them and a clean Dyson sphere identification would be (one of very few) undeniable signatures for intelligence. So, searches for Dyson spheres have been conducted – the IRAS infrared satellite covered 96% of the sky up to a distance of 300pc and found a few (16) ambiguous but interesting candidates over a million surveyed stars. So, Dyson spheres may not be as fictional as they may sound.

Like I previously said, Shkadov thrusters may sound a little too fictional, regardless, they too are theorized as an indication of a Type II civilization. They are a Class A stellar engine. These engines are stellar propulsion systems, consisting of an enormous mirror or light sail which would balance gravitational attraction towards and radiation pressure away from the star. Since the radiation pressure of the star would now be asymmetrical, the excess radiation pressure acts as net thrust accelerating the star. Such thrust and acceleration would be extremely small, but such a system could be stable for millennia and any planetary system attached to the star would be dragged along by its parent star. Class B stellar engines are essentially Dyson spheres i.e., they are only used as an energy source and do not generate thrust. Class C stellar engines also known as the Badescu-Cathcart engine combines the other two classes of engines employing both the propulsive aspects of the Shkadov thruster, and the energy generating aspects of a Dyson sphere. This type of engine would be extremely unstable though it would generate far more thrust than a Class A engine. Once again, these stellar engines would tend to have similar observable effects on their host galaxy/star as that of Dyson spheres with the added observational effect of seeing entire systems of stars and planets moving noticeably through space.

If we keep exploring (and extending) such ideas any further we will quickly start descending into what I would now consider undeniably science fiction territory, though who am I to say, given I’ve been trying to convince you all along that the search for extraterrestrial intelligence is in fact rational and something we must devote resources to. The only question I posed that I haven’t explicitly explored is an evaluation of our chances of finding extraterrestrial intelligence, but I think I’ve provided sufficient intuition for thinking about that throughout the paper.

Conclusion

To conclude, let’s assume we find an extraterrestrial intelligence. What happens next? Will history repeat itself like the Columbian exchange? This is exactly the question that Kwon10, Bercovici10, Cunningham10, and Varnum10 explored and found an answer to. Their work suggests that our reactions to a future confirmed discovery of extraterrestrial life/intelligence are likely to be fairly positive, and on that note, I will end my discussion with a brief summary.

The paper started off by presenting a variety of different views on how life may be defined only to conclude that it is a very complex process and one that is not especially relevant to this essay. However, it was primarily meant to make the case for why defining intelligence and searching for intelligence instead of life may be a more appealing endeavor. We then proceeded to define intelligence and through this process highlight the fact that for the purposes of SETI, intelligence really just means a sign of intelligence, which may be defined as any unnatural observation in the universe. Once a reasonable definition for what SETI meant had been established, the paper moved onto exploring our motivation to search for an extraterrestrial intelligence. Starting off with a philosophical argument, then briefly pausing to understand the scale, both in terms of space and time, of the universe and why that meant SETI was a perfectly rational endeavor and concluding with a more logical (albeit a little somber) argument, contrasting the initial philosophical one. 

At this point, we had established exactly what we were searching for and why we would be searching for it. So, it was time to move onto how we were doing this. This last section of the essay focused specifically on 3 of the primary approaches we take to search for extraterrestrial intelligence. Starting off with radio signatures, that are, by far, the most scientifically sound and concentrated effort we have made – the rationale for this is pulled from some of the most famous SETI papers while technical details are largely excluded for the sake of brevity. We then move onto techno signatures, two approaches are explored in reasonable depth, but the conclusion is clear – these approaches are not as mature as they need to be to justify investing billions of dollars into. Lastly, there is a brief segment on some of the most famous hypothetical theories and how we may identify extraterrestrial intelligences on the basis of those theories. With that, I hope this essay served its primary purpose which was to provide a brief analysis on what the Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence (SETI) is, what it is motivated by, what it entails, and what its chances of success are.
