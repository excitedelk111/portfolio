Design

Concentration Requirements

This article walks you through the process of analyzing and identifying usability flaws in an existing interface, creating low-fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes for various screen sizes, and building a responsive website based on those prototypes. I wanted to pick an interface that I knew very well and used quite often so that the effort I would be putting into redesigning it would be well worth it and so that I had a sufficiently large dataset of usability concerns that I could try to solve. So, I chose Brown's Computer Science Concentration Requirements page.

Automated Ordering Kiosks

This article walks you through the preparatory steps of creating personas and storyboards including contextual inquiries which involve observing real users interacting with an interface and interviewing them about their experiences. I will then use this information to develop two personas as well as a storyboard for one of these personas. I wanted to pick an interface that was in public view (i.e., so that other members of the public may see what is going on) so as to not make those being observed feel uncomfortable or surprised that someone could see what they were doing. At the same time, I did not want to pick something that only had very simple interactive components like a faucet or door handle. So I decided to go with an automated ordering kiosk at a cafe.

NimbleRx

This article walks you through the process of designing an interactive interface for an emerging startup when working in a team. It will cover the full process of mocking up a solution to the startup's concept. This process was roughly split into four parts to exemplify an iterative design process flow. The first was sketching ideas of the interface, followed by creating an interactive, high-fidelity prototype, then conducting user testing on a final, revised prototype, and lastly contacting the startup to show them the design we came up with. We wanted to pick a startup that was doing something meaningful and since one of our team members intends on becoming a doctor, we ended up choosing NimbleRx.

Chair

Over a 3 week period, we (a team of 3) worked through the ideate, prototype, test and evolve phases of the design process. We were provided with a PRD (Product Requirements Document) by a consumer research firm. During our design process, we produced 75+ sketches, 10+ low fidelity prototypes, used multiple decision making frameworks, created a BOM (Bill of Materials), FBD (Free Body Diagrams) for 2 different loads on the chair, detailed CAD models and extensive FEA simulation in Fusion 360 to test the structural rigidity of the chair. We concluded with a presentation to a former senior executive of multiple Fortune 200 companies. A more detailed article is coming soon!

Canvas

Over a 5 week period, we (a team of 2) mentored by a designer at Disney and guest lecturer at Stanford's d.school worked through the entire design process as we re-imagined the UI and UX of LMS software (Canvas). We first conducted user research across several schools including Brown, MIT, CMU and Stanford, and then synthesized that research to determine our project goals. We then had multiple rounds of ideation, and during prototyping, we created two iterations, conducting expert critique sessions, think-a-loud sessions with users, and iteration ranking surveys between the first and the second. We concluded with a final working Figma prototype. A more detailed article is coming soon!
